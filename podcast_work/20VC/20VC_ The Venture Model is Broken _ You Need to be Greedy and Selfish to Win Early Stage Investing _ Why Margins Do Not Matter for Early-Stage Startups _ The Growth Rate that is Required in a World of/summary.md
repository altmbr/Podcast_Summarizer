# 20VC: Gili Raanan, Founder @ Cyberstarts — Episode Summary

---

## 1. Key Themes

### The Venture Model is Structurally Broken for Most Participants
Gili's core thesis is that venture returns are deeply concentrated, and the current wave of inflated entry prices combined with stagnant unicorn production rates means capital destruction is coming for most players. The math simply doesn't work at scale.

> "The flow of new players into cybersecurity is quite steady for the past 20 years. You know, you're looking at around 350, 400 new teams that get funded every year... out of around 150 new companies in cybersecurity in Israel, the likelihood you'll hit a successful company is still one to one percent." [00:06:50]

> "I don't think it's going to work. I think it's going to end up with some serious catastrophe for many of the players. The market is not balanced. A lot of that cash that's flowing into the market would be wasted." [00:00:00]

### Growth Rate as the Single Most Predictive Signal
Gili argues that exceptional, organic growth velocity is the clearest signal of business health and is self-reinforcing — it becomes part of a company's DNA and doesn't fade without a major external shock.

> "Whenever a business is getting to a point it's growing super fast, it becomes part of their DNA. So it would not slow down just because of averages and things like that. There needs to be a significant external event to slow them down." [00:15:35]

> "If you go 4x, 4x, 3x and 3x on new ARR, not ARR, new ARR... that's 144x after five years, which means that in the first year, even if you have booked a million dollars of new ARR, in the fifth year, you'll book $144 million of new ARR." [00:26:27]

### Secondaries as a Structural Talent Retention Tool
Rather than viewing secondaries purely as LP liquidity events, Gili frames them as the primary mechanism to prevent talent flight from high-growth private companies. CyberStarts has created a recurring, programmatic employee liquidity fund to institutionalize this.

> "I'm not in the business of babysitting founders... The antidote for that market built-in weakness is the secondary... at CyberStarts, we created a vehicle. We call it employee liquidity fund, which is focused not on one-off type of secondary deals, but creating a program, a recurring program with a portfolio company, where we provide liquidity to their employees every year." [00:33:06]

## 2. Contrarian Perspectives

### Being Greedy and Selfish Are Virtues in Early-Stage Investing
Most people frame greed and selfishness as negative traits. Gili explicitly inverts this — arguing they are the correct orientations for early-stage venture, particularly around price discipline.

> "We need to be selfish and we need to be greedy. Those are good traits for an early stage investor. Those are not bad negative traits for anybody who's dealing with early stage. Price is an important consideration." [00:13:46]

### Gross Margins Are Irrelevant at the Early Stage
Against the prevailing wisdom that margin profile determines business quality, Gili argues that margins are a future-state concern that actively distracts founders from the right priorities right now.

> "How often I discuss gross margins with my early stage companies? Never... I would tell you, gross margins are important. Let's talk about it in 2029. And let's build the foundations of healthy business, assuming that we would get to deal with gross margins." [00:25:19]

### IPOs Are Marketing Events, Not Liquidity Events
Most founders and investors treat the IPO as the ultimate liquidity milestone. Gili argues the opposite — that IPOs are actually liquidity-restricting branding exercises, and the extension of private markets with secondaries is more functionally sound.

> "Going public is not a financial event. It's a branding event... It's hell for liquidity. But it's an important marketing event." [00:30:11]

### Too Much Capital Does Not Harm Great Companies
The conventional worry is that a capital-flush great company will defocus and bloat. Gili flatly rejects this, arguing it infantilizes founders and that the real danger is businesses with bad unit economics spending wastefully — not great companies getting extra runway.

> "I'm not in the business of babysitting founders. And for me, this is like babysitting the founder if we trust them to build, in my case, an important cybersecurity company that's critical to all the major banks in the U.S." [00:23:49]

### Focus on Your Winners, Not Your Misses
Against the Sequoia/a16z doctrine of obsessing over market share and mourning every miss, Gili argues that fixating on misses is psychologically and operationally destructive for fund performance.

> "I focus on the deals I've done and the teams I've partnered with. That's where I put my focus and energy. You know, you can't cover everything and you can't get everything. You are not going to win every battle." [00:41:22]

## 3. Companies Identified

**Wiz**
Cloud security company, one of the fastest-growing enterprise software companies ever. Mentioned as the defining proof point of what elite growth velocity looks like in cybersecurity.
> "At Wiz, when you look at the first year of selling software, the first quarter was a million dollar. And then second quarter of selling software was two million dollars. And then eight and then 24. That's an amazing year. That's in 2020." [00:16:22]

**Island**
Enterprise browser company, founded 2019 by Mike Fey (CEO) and Dan Amiga (CTO). Now valued at ~$5 billion. Mentioned as an example of a company that created and defined its own market category.
> "The company is basically selling browsers... Their idea is enterprise browser... the number of CISOs that told us that they need an enterprise browser equals the number of CIOs that told the market in 2006 that they need an iPhone. It's a market that doesn't exist. And still, the company is growing super fast. It's a $5 billion company today." [00:20:27]

**Sierra**
AI agent company. Mentioned as a counter-example of a company that stalled early (went to zero revenue for two quarters) then exploded to $12M new ARR in the next 12 months — illustrating that early flatness doesn't preclude explosive growth if founders diagnose and pivot correctly.
> "They had an amazing start... and they sold zero for two quarters. Literally zero... And the next 12 months, they sold 12 million dollars of new business." [00:17:22]

**No Name Security**
API security company, CyberStarts Fund 1 portfolio. Sold to Akamai for ~$500M. Mentioned as a cautionary tale of niche-market plateauing — even with strong early growth, a too-narrow market ceiling limits compounding.
> "They slowed down. Why? The market for API security, it wasn't a market. It was a niche segment within application security... And eventually, we sold the business to Akamai for half a billion dollars or so." [00:19:32]

## 5. Operating Insights

### Let Team Members Play to Their Strengths, Not Patch Their Weaknesses
Gili's most concrete partnership-building insight is that trying to standardize partners to a single operating model destroys their competitive edge. The right model is to identify each person's relative superpower and design their role around it.

> "I would let each team member play on their relative strengths and would not require them to focus on improving their weaknesses... on the things that are exceptional, they are creating real advantage, real greatness... That leaves CyberStarts with a team of people that really enjoy working with each other." [00:44:06]

### Prioritize Founder Chemistry Over Founder Credentials
In the last 12 months, Gili updated his view that chemistry with founders is the most important and underweighted investment criterion. The test is simple: shared history through adversity.

> "Founders chemistry. How important that is... Do they know each other? Were they, I don't know, roommates for a long period of time? Did they work together and went through some challenges?" [00:45:25]

### Sequence Your Company's Problems — Don't Solve Everything at Once
As a board member and investor, Gili explicitly helps founders bucket problems by time horizon: what to tackle now versus what to defer. This is how he avoids founder overwhelm and lets teams focus on what's actually company-defining in each year.

> "Part of our job as investors is to really help the founders realize what challenges, what problems they need to tackle right now, this year, 2026. And what are the challenges and problems that they would tackle in 2027 and 2028?" [00:25:47]

## 6. Overlooked Insights

### The Programmatic Employee Liquidity Fund is a New Asset Class Hiding in Plain Sight
Gili briefly mentions CyberStarts' "employee liquidity fund" — a vehicle that underwrites a recurring, annual tender offer for employees of portfolio companies. This was mentioned in passing but it is a genuinely novel fund structure. Most secondary activity is opportunistic and one-off. A fund that systematically commits to annual employee liquidity across a portfolio is creating a durable competitive moat for talent retention at the portfolio company level, while generating a recurring deal flow engine for the fund itself. It also implicitly solves the LP alignment problem (DPI without forcing founders to IPO or sell). This is a fundable strategy in its own right.

> "We created a vehicle. We call it employee liquidity fund... creating a program, a recurring program with a portfolio company, where we provide liquidity to their employees every year... The very same type of liquidity they would get in a public market, they would get it in a private company." [00:33:06]

### The Public Market Multiple Compression in Vertical SaaS May Signal AI Displacement Fear, Not Valuation Irrationality
Gili briefly and tentatively offers an explanation for why strong-growth public SaaS companies like Monday (1.5x ARR) and Wix (2.5x ARR) are trading at historically low multiples. He suggests the market is pricing in an expectation that autonomous AI programs will eat their business models. This was glossed over quickly, but if true, it has enormous implications: the public market is already discounting the next wave of AI-native competitors against today's SaaS incumbents, and private investors building in those categories should take that signal seriously before writing checks at 100x ARR.

> "My guess is there is an expectation that autonomous programs would displace and would eat part of the business of those companies... if those companies would continue, regardless of the market, would continue to grow at the incredible pace, the multipliers would rebound back to where they are. The multiplier is just the market anticipation for your growth rate." [00:29:13]
