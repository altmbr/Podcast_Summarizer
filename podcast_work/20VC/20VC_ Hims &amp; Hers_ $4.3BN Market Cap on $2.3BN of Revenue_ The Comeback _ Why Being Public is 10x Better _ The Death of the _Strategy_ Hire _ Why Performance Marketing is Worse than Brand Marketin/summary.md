# 20VC: Andrew Dudum (Hims & Hers) — Podcast Summary

## 1. Key Themes

### The Public Markets as a Competitive Advantage, Not a Burden
Andrew makes the provocative case that being public is actually *better* for building a great company than staying private. The 90-day accountability cycle, transparency of vision, and the forced discipline of proving progress attract top talent and keep the team sharp.

> "If you're a highly competitive person, you get to put out high benchmarks every 90 days and see if you can actually deliver on it... When you're private, it's so easy to get cozy." — Andrew Dudum [00:03:49]

### Hims & Hers as a Healthcare Disruption Platform, Not a DTC Brand
Andrew consistently reframes Hims not as a consumer brand but as a systemic disruptor of how healthcare is distributed in America — shifting power away from PBMs, insurance, and drug companies toward the consumer, with price transparency and on-demand access.

> "I don't think hims is a DTC company. I think hims is disrupting how healthcare is delivered in a consumer focused fashion... Instead of going through the PBMs and then through insurance and then through reimbursements... all of it, or as much of it as possible, will go through consumer channels like hims and hers." — Andrew Dudum [00:48:25]

### Preventative Healthcare as the Core Long-Term Business Model
The long arc of Hims is not selling treatments — it's owning the preventative front door to healthcare. Free or near-free diagnostics act as a loss leader to acquire patients early and build a lifelong relationship. They are verticalizing lab infrastructure (acquired YourBioHealth) to make this economical.

> "My goal, my vision is very quickly I want to give that away as a part of the hims and hers membership for free... That is the vision, a preventative front door that is near cost or free." — Andrew Dudum [00:40:28]

## 2. Contrarian Perspectives

### The "Strategy Hire" Is Almost Always a Mistake
Against the prevailing wisdom that scaling companies need to "upgrade" to experienced, credentialed executives, Andrew argues this is one of the most common and costly mistakes founders make.

> "I think as you scale, there's a pressure to go hire that big strategy person who has more credentials than you... I think you have to fight that at all costs. I've made mistakes. I've hired people like that because I thought it was the right move. And I've always come back to find people who love the mission, who love to build, who are tactically excellent." — Andrew Dudum [00:08:19]

### CAC Does Not Always Go Up — Scale Can Reverse It
The conventional wisdom in DTC is that customer acquisition cost only ever increases. Andrew explicitly disagrees.

> "Disagree. At scale, I think with scale, with assortment, with brand value, new channels become unlocked, new efficiency grows, leverage accelerates with assortment. It often only goes one way, but I don't think always." — Andrew Dudum [00:54:28]

### Brand Marketing Only Works With Repetitive Consistency — "One-Off" Campaigns Are Largely Wasted Money
Most companies treat brand marketing as episodic and creative. Andrew argues that the creative novelty is actually the enemy of effectiveness — consistent repetition of the same message is what builds real brand equity.

> "The thing early companies struggle with when it comes to communications and their brand is they get bored of saying the same thing... What makes great brands great is the fact that everybody knows why they exist, why they fight, who their customer is... because they say the same damn thing in 20 different ways every single week." — Andrew Dudum (quoting Kathy, Chief Comms Officer) [00:35:16]

### Being First to Market Is Overrated — Being Best Is What Matters
The startup culture fetishizes speed-to-market. Andrew has deliberately evolved away from this, choosing to enter categories later with higher quality and clinical confidence.

> "My net, net, net takeaway is I don't think we actually need to be first ever in market. I want to be best in market." — Andrew Dudum [00:16:13]

### Physical Infrastructure Is the True AI Moat
While most investors debate software and LLM moats, Andrew argues that businesses requiring physical infrastructure and licensed specialization are actually the most defensible in the AI age.

> "Some of the most defensible businesses in the age of Anthropic and OpenAI are businesses that actually do something physical. They require actual specialization and they require infrastructure." — Andrew Dudum [00:23:37]

## 3. Companies Identified

**Eucalyptus**
Global telehealth platform operating in Australia, UK, Germany, and Japan. Acquired by Hims & Hers for approximately $1.5B. Mentioned as the dominant player in international direct-to-consumer healthcare markets.

> "Tim was the best operator overseas. No question. I mean, his understanding of the customer, his ability to build a team that just moves fast, experiments, is willing to take risks and fail... Cancer detection company offering a blood test that can screen for 50-100 cancers, with particular strength in hard-to-detect cancers like pancreatic, ovarian, and prostate. Hims & Hers has partnered to distribute the test at ~$600.

> "It's called the Gallery Test by Grail. It's a blood test and it can detect 50 to 100 cancers... This blood test was thousands of dollars for the last few years. **Prenuvo**
Full-body MRI scanning company offering preventative cancer and disease detection. Andrew personally uses it annually and has partnered with them.

> "I take Prenuvo annually. So I recommend it to my family... The idea that this cost could come down dramatically is very real. 500K machine capitalized over a decade. The cost could come down to something like $300 a year to do this test eventually." — Andrew Dudum [00:22:38]

**YourBioHealth**
At-home blood collection device company acquired by Hims & Hers. The device uses 30 micro-needles to painlessly collect blood at home, enabling cheap lab processing for comprehensive biomarker panels.

> "Last year we acquired YourBioHealth, which is one of the few at-home blood collection devices... This device costs just a couple bucks to manufacture and you can click it on your arm... a full panel, 50 biomarkers, and it will cost us like almost nothing." — Andrew Dudum [00:39:31]

## 5. Operating Insights

### Replace Yourself Every 12 Months or You Will Stagnate
Andrew has a disciplined framework: your job as a leader changes every 12 months as the company scales. The only way to move up to the next highest-leverage focus is to consciously replace yourself in your current role with someone equal or better.

> "If you want to continue to scale in our organization or any organization that's growing, you have to realize that your job is changing every 12 months to level yourself up to the next highest leverage focus area. In order to do that, you have to replace yourself every 12 months with talent equal or better always." — Andrew Dudum [00:09:45]

### Hire for Grit and Crisis Experience Over Credentials
When building a disruption-oriented team, deliberately seek out executives who have navigated extreme business crises. This is a repeatable hiring filter, not just a philosophical preference. Andrew identifies specific crisis archetypes (Uber COVID, Robinhood GameStop) as proxies for the grit he needs.

> "I seek out a lot of grit because I think when you're disrupting an industry... you have to have a team that is used to being uncomfortable and used to getting through it and used to staying calm and having that resilience." — Andrew Dudum [00:06:58]

### Being Uncomfortable Is a Leading Indicator of Pushing Hard Enough
Andrew has operationalized discomfort as a management signal. If the leadership team is not feeling the stress of being close to the edge, they are not pushing hard enough. He actively teaches this to his team.

> "What I've found, at least in running this business, is if you don't feel like you are getting close to that line, you're probably not pushing hard enough." — Andrew Dudum [00:29:54]

### Loss-Leader Diagnostics as a Customer Acquisition and Retention Engine
Lab testing is intentionally run at cost (or below) as a strategic platform benefit — not a profit center. This is a specific operational playbook: use diagnostic access as the low-friction entry point to a long-term patient relationship, then monetize through treatment over years.

> "My least profitable product that is most important right now is the lab testing offering... It's because I believe the loss leader value to the patient is incredibly important." — Andrew Dudum [00:46:16]

## 6. Overlooked Insights

### Lipoprotein(a) Testing as a Mass Market Opportunity Hidden in Plain Sight
Andrew briefly tells a story about a friend with a Lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)] score of 450 (should be under 70) — a genetic cardiovascular risk marker that is almost never tested in standard panels, yet predicts heart attack risk decades in advance. He mentions it almost as an anecdote, but what he is actually describing is a massive, underdiagnosed public health problem with a specific, measurable biomarker that is cheap to test for and could be deployed at scale through Hims' platform. For investors, any company building around Lp(a) testing, treatment (several drugs in clinical trials), or awareness is sitting on a large unmet need. This is not a widely known consumer-facing category yet.

> "Have you done testing for this specific genetic risk factor for heart disease? It's called lipoprotein little a. He's like, no, I've never done that test. So he ran, he did that test and he came back and he's like, my number is like 450. I was like, well, that's a problem because that number should be like under 70. And if it's 450, it means there's a pretty good chance you're going to have a heart attack at like 50 or 60." — Andrew Dudum [00:42:05]

### The New Patient Cohort Has No Legacy Healthcare Relationship — Whoever Gets There First Wins Decades
Andrew very briefly makes the point that the majority of Hims patients are young people who moved to a new city, have no existing doctor, and are entering the healthcare system *for the first time* through Hims. This is not just a customer acquisition insight — it is a structural generational shift. If Hims (or a competitor) captures the healthcare relationship at age 22-25, that relationship could span 50+ years. The lifetime value economics of this cohort have almost certainly not been fully priced into the market. No one on the call paused on this.

> "They moved to a new city, they moved to New York and they're 22 and they're like, I don't have a doctor yet. I'm feeling a little sad. What do I do? And their first time entry into the healthcare system is hims and hers. And so the wave of healthcare for the future, for the next 20 years, for the 50 years after that, is going to be a patient population that's starting today." — Andrew Dudum [00:50:09]
