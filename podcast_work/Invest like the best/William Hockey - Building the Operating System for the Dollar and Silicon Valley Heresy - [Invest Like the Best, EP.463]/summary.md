## 1. Key Themes

### The Regulated Bank as an Unfair Moat in Software

Column is fundamentally differentiated because it combines software with an actual bank charter — something most competitors cannot replicate. This creates regulatory and structural barriers that pure-play fintechs simply cannot overcome. The business earns 90%+ of revenue from software (per-API-call SaaS), while passing most banking economics downstream to customers.

> "We are technically a bank, but unlike banks, we make 90 plus percent of our money off of software. And so similar to any SaaS company, it's a per API call. It's a pure play tech business." — William Hockey [00:06:30]

> "We bought a regulated bank during the first Biden administration. And that required us not grow or like not focus on revenue for two, three years and do a bunch of stuff that inherently didn't scale... There's just no way that could have happened [with VC money]." — William Hockey [00:23:01]

### Bootstrapping at Scale: A Structural Advantage, Not a Constraint

Hockey deliberately chose to fund Column entirely himself (via pledged Plaid shares as debt collateral), which allowed multi-year investment in infrastructure that would have been unfundable by VCs. The model creates employee alignment, eliminates preference stack dilution, provides annual liquidity through share buybacks, and enables long-term decision-making free from fundraising cycles.

> "VC money is kind of like heroin. It feels good. It's amazing. But like, you got to keep shooting up... I know very few people that have raised like $100 million Series A and then they're like, I'm done." — William Hockey [00:21:45]

> "What I can tell people is our profits every year. Imagine that's like our funding round. Each year, like on January 1st, we raise a massive round each year... By the way, you also haven't diluted at all. By the way, you also don't have any pref stack." — William Hockey [00:25:18]

### The Dollar as Global Operating System and National Security Asset

Hockey frames the U.S. dollar not just as a currency but as an instrument of geopolitical power — one that precedes military force. Understanding this is central to Column's global strategy, particularly in emerging markets where dollarization is high. Countries that distrust their own central banks are the most dependent on U.S. financial infrastructure.

> "The reason we were able to go to Venezuela is because we had fundamentally destroyed the economy before. How? Sanctions... You can enforce American dominance without putting boots on the ground." — William Hockey [00:57:04]

> "China and Russia... that trade is still denominated for a vast majority in the U.S. dollar. That's mind-boggling... Russia doesn't want Chinese currency. China definitely doesn't want the ruble. They almost hate each other as much as they hate us. And the fact that they then choose a dollar is still so critical." — William Hockey [00:55:32]

## 2. Contrarian Perspectives

### Silicon Valley Is the Most Consensus Place on Earth — And That's a Vulnerability

Most people view SF/SV as a hotbed of radical thinking. Hockey argues it's actually the most intellectually uniform place in the world, alongside Beijing, which leads builders to build for each other rather than the world.

> "He says the two most consensus societies he's ever been to is San Francisco and Beijing... San Francisco is probably the most consensus place I've ever been to." — William Hockey [00:07:22]

> "Silicon Valley is not a popular place, and I think we tend to forget that. We think we're on the top of the world, but I don't know what our approval ratings are, but I think they're probably pretty dang low. And I think that's for good reason." — William Hockey [00:18:25]

### Early Employees Take More Risk Than Founders — And We've Made It Too Safe to Start Companies

The conventional narrative glorifies founder risk. Hockey inverts this: early employees are actually the more economically exposed party, sacrificing salary, lifestyle, and career optionality. Meanwhile, founders face minimal real downside thanks to the safety net of the VC ecosystem, which is producing risk-averse companies.

> "An early stage employee takes way more risks than an early stage founder... I'm going to live with my friends instead of living by myself... But as a founder, you're not. It's a much higher likelihood of the next round, regardless of your company, you'll be able to sell some secondary." — William Hockey [00:34:36]

> "Starting companies are just too fucking safe. And it's caused a lot of companies to be just super safe companies. Like, hey, we're going to pivot to AI, wrap OpenAI, wrap anthropic, whatever. Like that's not bold. That's not ambitious." — William Hockey [00:35:34]

### The YC Request for Startups Is a List of What NOT to Build

Widely considered a useful signal for where to focus, Hockey argues it's actually a contrarian inverse indicator. By the time something is consensus enough to appear on YC's list, it's already flooded with capital and talent, eliminating alpha.

> "YC puts out this request for startups. My recommendation is like that should be a list of startups you should not start. Because by the time that gets so consensus that this is a good area or super interesting, the amount of capital and the amount of smart people is like moth the light." — William Hockey [01:07:41]

### U.S. Financial Infrastructure Is Actually Good — The Problem Is Implementation, Not Technology

The prevailing narrative is that legacy financial rails are broken, outdated, and ripe for disruption. Hockey pushes back, arguing the Fed's systems are fast, reliable, and already capable of real-time settlement — the constraints are business model and regulatory, not technological.

> "The Fed has a pretty good tech team. Their systems are actually pretty good... We've had that for decades. Systems are very good, extremely reliant. They're fantastic... The problem isn't in the fundamental infrastructure. It's in our implementation of it." — William Hockey [01:00:50]

### The Biggest AI Beneficiaries Are Fat, Inefficient Legacy Brands — Not AI Companies

Counter to the consensus view that AI-native startups will capture AI value, Hockey argues that large incumbents with massive distribution and bloated cost structures (especially banks) will be the primary beneficiaries — similar to how Standard Oil, not the railroads themselves, captured railroad value.

> "The value in railroads accrued to the oil companies... What is the equivalent area for AI?... I think like the biggest, fattest, most inefficient brands are even the best beneficiary of AI. Because brands have a massive moat and man, there's a lot of costs to cut there." — William Hockey [01:03:25]

## 5. Operating Insights

### Annual Employee Tender as Retention and Alignment Tool

Hockey runs an annual share buyback program — 25% of earnings — that provides employees real liquidity without needing an IPO or acquisition. This addresses the fundamental tension between long-term equity value and short-term life needs (housing, school, lifestyle), dramatically reducing regretted attrition.

> "What we do every single year, we take 25% of our earnings and we just buy back our shares with employees. So we just run our own tender every single year. It's been great for retention. It's able to actually get liquidity to employees when they need it." — William Hockey [00:23:54]

### Housing Proximity Subsidy as a Scalability Trade-off Worth Making

Column pays employees $2,000/month toward rent or mortgage if they live within two miles of the office. This is explicitly non-scalable by Silicon Valley norms — but the density, culture, and retention it creates may be worth more than the dollars spent.

> "We pay employees like, here's $2,000 a month to go to a rent or mortgage if you live two miles with the office... Is that going to be a good use of venture capital? Probably not. So we can just do all this stuff from an employee perspective, from a retention perspective." — William Hockey [00:23:30]

### Target Second-Time Employees, Not New Grads, to Sell Non-Standard Equity Stories

The preference/dilution math of conventional VC equity is opaque to new grads but immediately legible to someone who's been through multiple funding rounds and pivots. Column deliberately recruits people at their second job, where the structural advantages of clean cap tables, no pref stack, and liquidity are viscerally real.

> "We target people that this is their second company... It's a hard story for a 21-year-old. It's actually a pretty easy story for a 25-year-old that's been through six rounds and four pivots at their previous company and don't have anything to show for it." — William Hockey [00:26:14]

### Becoming the Best in the World Requires Finding the Most Boring Problem Fascinating

Hockey's operating philosophy of dominance is rooted in deep, obsessive, multi-decade specialization in areas that most people find too boring to pursue — including reading a 2,000-page book on 19th century Chinese banking to extract a single idea worth millions.

> "One of the best determiners for success of founders is can they find the most boring thing humanly possible interesting? And can they find that interesting over a multi-decade period?... Those places are really boring... You have to suffer in silence for a huge amount of time." — William Hockey [00:39:48]

## 6. Overlooked Insights

### Emerging Market Banks Are Talent Compounders That U.S. Fintechs Cannot Access

Hockey briefly mentions something profound: in emerging markets, the best talent — which is equally distributed globally by biology — has nowhere to go except the dominant local employers (banks, breweries). This means emerging market bank executive teams may actually be higher quality than their Western counterparts, and these institutions can out-innovate on verticalization because they face no competition from specialized software vendors. This is a massively underappreciated competitive dynamic that also creates an acquisition/partnership angle for any company wanting access to that talent.

> "I'd say the mid-level and top, can actually be quite a bit higher than people there. I'd say you take your emerging markets bank executive team, their hands down is way better than I think probably what you see in the Western world... There's no Anthropic to go to. They don't have the ability to move to London and go to DeepMind." — William Hockey [00:13:34]

### The DRC Is Quietly Becoming One of the World's Most Important Economic Nodes

Kinshasa is on track to be the world's largest city within a decade, is a dominant exporter of critical metals essential to the AI/EV supply chain, and is heavily financed by Chinese capital. Yet Hockey notes that 95% of Silicon Valley cannot identify what country it is the capital of. This represents a massive blind spot: the combination of explosive population growth, critical mineral dominance, Chinese financial entanglement, and extreme dollarization makes the DRC one of the highest-optionality emerging market bets for financial infrastructure players — and it is almost entirely uncontested by U.S. technology companies.

> "It's going to be the largest city in the world in probably five to ten years... CRC is one of the largest exporters, not the largest exporter of some critical metals in the world... There's a lot of Chinese investment... 95% of people in Silicon Valley couldn't tell you what Kinshasa is the capital of." — William Hockey [00:09:08]
